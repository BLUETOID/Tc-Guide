Verbal vs Non-verbal Communication Unit 1

Aspect Verbal Communication Non-verbal Communication
Medium Words (spoken or written) Body language, gestures, expressions
Explicitness Explicit and direct Implicit and subtle
Control Easier to control Often unconscious and harder to control
Ambiguity Less ambiguous (when clear) More open to interpretation
Impact 7% of message impact (Mehrabian) 55% body language + 38% tone = 93%
Examples Presentations, emails, reports Eye contact, posture, facial expressions

Formal vs Informal Communication Unit 1

Aspect Formal Communication Informal Communication
Channel Official, designated channels Unofficial, casual channels
Documentation Usually documented/recorded Rarely documented
Speed Slower due to protocols Faster and spontaneous
Language Standard, professional Casual, colloquial
Examples Memos, reports, official meetings Grapevine, lunch conversations

Communication Flow Directions Unit 1

Direction Description Examples
Downward From superiors to subordinates; flows down the hierarchy Instructions, policies, feedback, job assignments
Upward From subordinates to superiors; flows up the hierarchy Reports, suggestions, grievances, feedback
Horizontal/Lateral Between peers at the same hierarchical level Team coordination, peer discussions, collaboration
Diagonal/Cross-functional Across different departments and levels Project teams, cross-departmental collaboration

Formal Communication Networks Unit 1

Network Type Structure Characteristics Best For
Chain Network A → B → C → D → E Linear, hierarchical; message passes through each member Formal reporting, military organizations
Wheel Network All spokes connect to central hub One central leader controls all communication Quick decisions, centralized control
Circle Network Each member connects to two neighbors Equal participation; no single leader Team collaboration, brainstorming
Y Network Combination of chain with branches Has a central position but less centralized than wheel Organizations with multiple reporting lines
All-Channel (Star) Everyone connected to everyone Maximum interaction; no hierarchy Problem-solving, creative teams

Encoding vs Decoding Unit 1

Process Description Performed By
Encoding Converting thoughts, ideas, or feelings into a transmittable format (words, symbols, gestures) Sender/Source
Decoding Interpreting the received message back into thoughts and meaning Receiver/Audience

Vowel Sounds vs Consonant Sounds Unit 1

Aspect Vowel Sounds Consonant Sounds
Production Open vocal tract, no obstruction Obstruction or constriction in vocal tract
Voicing All vowels are voiced Can be voiced or voiceless
Role in Syllable Form the nucleus/core of syllables Form the margins of syllables
Count in English 20 vowel sounds (12 pure + 8 diphthongs) 24 consonant sounds
Examples /iː/ (see), /æ/ (cat), /ɑː/ (car) /p/ (pen), /t/ (top), /s/ (sit)
Classification By Tongue height, position, lip rounding Place, manner, voicing

Networking at Career Fairs: Dos and Don'ts Unit 2

Do's Don'ts
Research companies beforehand Don't arrive unprepared without knowing companies
Dress professionally and appropriately Don't dress too casually or sloppily
Bring multiple copies of your resume Don't forget or run out of resumes
Prepare a 30-second elevator pitch Don't ramble or speak without focus
Ask thoughtful questions about the company Don't ask questions you could easily Google
Make eye contact and offer a firm handshake Don't avoid eye contact or give weak handshake
Collect business cards and take notes Don't forget names or key conversation points
Follow up within 24-48 hours Don't wait too long to follow up or never follow up
Be polite to everyone including other candidates Don't be rude or dismissive to anyone
Show enthusiasm and genuine interest Don't appear bored, distracted, or arrogant

Report vs Proposal Unit 3

Aspect Report Proposal
Purpose Documents what has been done or found Suggests what should be done in future
Timing Written after an event, study, or project Written before a project begins
Focus Presents findings, analysis, conclusions Presents plans, solutions, requests
Tone Informative and objective Persuasive and convincing
Content Data, results, recommendations Problem, solution, budget, timeline
Audience Response Reader gains knowledge/understanding Reader approves, funds, or decides
Example Project completion report, research report Business proposal, research grant proposal

Aspect Elevator Pitch Business Pitch

Purpose Spark interest, get a meeting Secure funding, partnership, or approval
Detail Level High-level overview only Comprehensive with data and projections
Visual Aids None Slide deck, demos, prototypes

Outcome Exchange contact info, schedule meeting Investment decision, contract signing

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs Unit 5

Level Need Category Description Examples
5 (Top) Self-Actualization Achieving full potential, creativity Personal growth, pursuing passions
4 Esteem Needs Respect, recognition, achievement Status, accomplishments, confidence
3 Love/Belonging Relationships, connection, community Friendship, family, intimacy
2 Safety Needs Security, stability, protection Job security, health, home safety
1 (Base) Physiological Needs Basic survival requirements Food, water, shelter, sleep
Limitations of Maslow's Theory
  • Hierarchy is not rigid—people may pursue higher needs before lower ones are fully met
  • Cultural differences may alter the priority of needs
  • Self-actualization is subjective and hard to measure
  • The theory is difficult to test empirically
  • Some needs can be pursued simultaneously
